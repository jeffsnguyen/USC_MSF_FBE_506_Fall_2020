For continuous functions defined by \(y= f(x)\), the point \((x_0,f(x_0))\) is a maxima or minima if \(x_0\) is in the domain of the function and \(f'(x) = 0\). Thus, for the below, we find and test extrema values \(x_0\) for which \(f'(x_0) = 0\).

a. \(y = x^2 - 6x + 5\)

This is a polynomial function, thus, it is continuous everywhere. We know:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f'(x_0) & = 0 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} & = 0 \\ 2x - 6 & = 0 \\ x & = 3 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Thus, we have the critical point \(x_0 = 3\)

Graph of the function:

curve(x^2 - 6*x + 5, -100,100)

Testing for min, max: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f'(x_0-1) & = f'(2) \qquad & f'(x_0+1) & = f'(4) \\ f'(x_0-1) & = 2(2) - 6 & f'(x_0+1) & = 2(4) - 6 \\ f'(x_0-1) & = -2 < 0 & f'(x_0+1) & = 2 > 0 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Finding \(f(3)\): \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f(3) & = 3^2 - 6(3) + 5 \\ f(3) & = -4 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

We see that the function is decreasing on \((-\infty,3)\), and increasing on \((3,\infty)\) Thus, the absolute minima of the function is at \((3,-4)\)

Finding inflection point: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f''(x_0) & = 0 \\ \frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} & = 0 \\ 2 & = 0 \text{ (not possible)} \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Thus the inflection point does not exist for this function.

Finding \(f(2.574355)\): \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} f(2.574355) & = \ln{\frac{2.574355+1}{(2.574355)^2+3}} \\ f(2.574355) & \approx -0.9908185 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

From the test, we see that the function curvature changes from concave to convex at \((2.574355,-0.9908185)\), thus \((2.574355,-0.9908185)\) is an inflection point.

The given production function is \(Q = 100N - N^2\)

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} Q'(N_0) & = 0 \\ \frac{dQ}{dN} & = 0 \\ -2N + 100 & = 0 \\ N & = 50 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Thus we have the critical point \(N_0 = 50\)

Graph of the function:

At \(p = 45\): \(q = 100 - (1.6)(45) = 28\) We know the price elasticity of demand _p is given by:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \epsilon_p & = \frac{dq}{dp}.\frac{p}{q} \\ & = (-1.6).\frac{45}{28} \\ & = -\frac{18}{7} \\ \approx -2.571429 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

b. \(q = \frac{100}{p^3}\), when price is p = $20.00

At \(p = 20\): \(q = \frac{100}{20^3} = \frac{1}{80}\) We know the price elasticity of demand _p is given by:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \epsilon_p & = \frac{dq}{dp}.\frac{p}{q} \\ & = \frac{-300}{p^4}.\frac{20}{\frac{1}{80}} \\ & = \frac{-300(20)(80)}{20^4} \\ & = -3 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

At \(p = 20.30\) and \(y = 5800\): \(q = 10.(20.30)^{-1.8}.(5800)^{.6} \approx 8.027197\) We know the price elasticity of demand _p is given by:

Question 5

b. Average Total Cost:

The average total cost is the total cost divided by quantity of output, i.e.: \(ATC = \frac{TC}{q} = \frac{10}{q} + 26 - 3.5q + \frac{1}{3}q^2\) Graph of the function:

atc <- function(x) (10/x) + 26 - 3.5*x + (1/3)*x^2
plot(x, atc(x), type = 'l', xlab = 'Output (q)', ylab = 'Average Total Cost (ATC)', main = 'Average Total Costs as a Function of Output', col = 'blue')

c.Average Variable Cost:

The total variable cost is the total cost less the fixed cost, i.e.costs that are not affected by quantity of output. The average total cost is the total variable cost divided by quantity of output. Thus: \(AVC = \frac{TVC}{q} = \frac{TC - FC}{q} = \frac{26q - 3.5q^2 + \frac{1}{3}q^3}{q} = 26 - 3.5q + \frac{1}{3}q^2\) Graph of the function:

avc <- function(x) 26 - 3.5*x + (1/3)*x^2
plot(x, atc(x), type = 'l', xlab = 'Output (q)', ylab = 'Average Variable Cost (AVC)', main = 'Average Variable Costs as a Function of Output', col = 'red')

d.Marginal Cost:

The marginal cost is the change in total cost relative to the change in quantity of output.

Thus: \[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} MC(q) & = \frac{\Deta_TC}{\Delta_q} \\ & = c'(q) \\ & = \frac{d_TC}{dq} \\ & = 26 - 7q + q^2 \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Graph of the function:

plot(x, mc(x), type = 'l', xlab = 'Output (q)', ylab = 'Marginal Cost (MC)', main = 'Marginal Costs as a Function of Output', col = 'orange')
